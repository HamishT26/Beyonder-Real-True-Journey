proofs where possible.

●​ DID identifier – A URI in the did:freed method namespace (e.g.
did:freed:example:1234abcdef). Each DID resolves to a DID Document

●​ Controller – The entity (person, organisation, AI agent) that exercises control
over the DID. Controllers maintain private keys that correspond to public keys in
the DID Document.
●​ Service endpoints – URLs or URNs describing services associated with the DID
(e.g. credential registry, profile storage).
●​ Verifiable credentials – Digital attestations about the subject (e.g. citizenship,
age, certifications), signed by trusted issuers.

3. DID Method: freed

and deactivated. The freed method operates as follows:

1.​ Creation – A new DID is generated by the controller using a keypair (e.g.
Ed25519). The controller submits a creation transaction to the Freed ID ledger
containing the DID Document with its public keys and service endpoints.
2.​ Resolution – Resolving a did:freed identifier queries the ledger and returns the
associated DID Document. Clients verify that the ledger proof matches the DID.
3.​ Update – To add or rotate keys or update service endpoints, the controller signs
an update message and submits it to the ledger. The update is appended to the
DID’s history.
4.​ Deactivation – The controller can deactivate its own DID by signing a
deactivation request. Deactivated DIDs remain resolvable but are marked as
inactive.

1.​ Issuance – Trusted issuers (e.g. governments, universities, AI ethics boards) sign
verifiable credentials and deliver them to the subject’s credential registry service
endpoint.
2.​ Presentation – When the subject needs to prove a claim, they present selected
credentials to a verifier. Presentations may use selective disclosure and
zero‑knowledge proofs to reveal only necessary attributes.
3.​ Verification – Verifiers check the issuer’s signature against the issuer’s DID
Document and ensure the credential has not been revoked.
4.​ Revocation – Issuers can revoke a credential by publishing a revocation entry in
a publicly accessible revocation registry. Holders and verifiers consult this
registry during verification.

●​ Ethics audits – Periodic audits ensure that issuers and verifiers adhere to agreed
ethical guidelines. Violators may have their issuance privileges suspended.

6. Interoperability
The Freed ID system is compatible with the W3C DID Core specification and Verifiable
Credentials Data Model. This means Freed ID identities and credentials can
interoperate with other self‑sovereign identity ecosystems, while retaining the additional

The Quantum to Classical Information Transmuter (QCIT) is a conceptual engine
designed to bridge the gap between quantum computation and classical computation
within the Trinity Hybrid‑AI. Inspired by the quantum energy transmutation engine, QCIT
“transmutes” the rich, phase‑coherent information processed by quantum modules into
a classical form that can be stored, analysed and acted upon by conventional hardware
without losing essential meaning.

1. Motivation
Quantum processors encode information in the amplitudes and phases of qubit states.
While quantum circuits can perform operations that would be intractable classically, their
outputs are probabilistic and inherently fragile. QCIT aims to extract useful classical
summaries from quantum states while preserving as much information as possible
about the underlying computation.

2. Architecture

1.​ Quantum Sampling Layer
2.​ Repeatedly measure the quantum register in different bases to build up statistics
of outcome probabilities, expectation values and correlation functions.
3.​ Use error‑mitigation techniques to correct for noise and decoherence.
4.​ Feature Extraction Layer
5.​ From sampled data, compute classical features such as:
o​ Probability distributions over measurement outcomes.
o​ Expectation values of observables (e.g., Pauli operators).
o​ Correlation matrices capturing entanglement patterns.
6.​ Optionally apply compressed sensing to reconstruct sparse representations of
quantum states.
7.​ Semantic Compression Layer
8.​ Use dimensionality reduction (PCA, autoencoders) or symbolic regression to
distil the extracted features into a lower‑dimensional representation that
preserves functional relationships.
9.​ Map complex amplitude patterns to interpretable quantities like energy spectra,
phase transitions or decision boundaries.
10.​Classical Encoding Layer
11.​Encode the compressed features into data structures compatible with classical
modules (e.g., JSON objects, tensors).

12.​Attach metadata about measurement context, fidelity and confidence intervals.
13.​Feedback Loop
14.​Provide feedback to the quantum module on which observables or measurement
bases are most informative, enabling adaptive sampling strategies.
15.​Update error‑mitigation parameters based on the quality of classical
reconstructions.

3. Use Cases

●​ Quantum simulation – Summarise the results of quantum simulations (e.g.,
chemistry, materials) into classical approximations that guide classical
optimisation algorithms.
●​ Quantum optimisation – Translate quantum‑annealer outputs into classical
candidate solutions for further refinement.
●​ Quantum machine learning – Convert quantum‑encoded embeddings into
classical feature vectors for downstream neural networks.

qubits. QCIT should incorporate variance‑reduction techniques to make
transmutation feasible on near‑term hardware.
●​ Information loss – Since measurement collapses quantum states, QCIT cannot
preserve full phase information. The goal is to retain task‑relevant information
while discarding superfluous degrees of freedom.
